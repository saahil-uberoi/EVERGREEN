About Grasslands

Grassland is a vegetation area dominated by almost continuously covered grass, and it is also one of the most widely distributed main vegetation types in the world. Usually, natural grassland can be considered as the middle part of the environmental gradient, with the forest at one end and desert at the other end. The grassland plays an important balancing role for the earth's environment. When Europeans first arrived in western Victoria, much of the extensive undulating plains they saw were covered with native grasslands. However, starting from the 1830s, the local grasslands were quickly occupied because grazing there did not require much effort. Soon after, large areas of native grassland in Victoria were completely replaced by introduced pastures, crops and weeds. The Victoria Volcanic Plain (VVP), located from the west of Melbourne to the border of South Australia, is the largest natural grassland nowadays, but compared to the pre-European colonial era, it has only 5% of the area left.

Grassland Ecosystem of Victoria Volcanic Plain

The natural temperate grassland of the Victoria Volcanic Plain is a highly fragmented and critically endangered ecological community. It is well known that the largest native animal on the Australian grassland is the kangaroo, the biggest of which is the red kangaroo, characteristic of the natural grassland in the dry inland areas. But because of the arrival of European immigrants, mammals introduced from other continents have become equally common. These animals include domestic animals, especially cattle and sheep, as well as a series of wild animals such as camels, horses, donkeys and goats. The biggest natural enemy to the grassland is the European rabbit, they are widely distributed, have strong reproductive ability, and their destructive power is also amazing. However, Australia does not have large predators, so the main predator is a dingo. There are very diverse reptiles, especially lizards, and various birds including large flightless emu, and various parrots.

Threats to biodiversity in Victorian Grassland

The interactions between native species are altered or destroyed by these invasive species, and climate change, Pollution, Overexploitation, and deforestation are also the major cause of extinction and can result in the loss of native biodiversity. The fewer species we have on the earth, the fewer humans we will have.
